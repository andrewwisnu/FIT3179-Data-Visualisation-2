Shinkansen, or more commonly known in english as the "Bullet Train", is a network of high-speed railway lines in Japan. Started its life from the capital city of Japan, Tokyo, to help Japan's economic growth and development by connecting other distant Japanese Prefectures.

Currently, it's network has expanded throughout 30 out of 47 Prefectures in Japan with more lines currently under development. This visualisation gives an overview of interesting statistics span throughout its more than half a decade lifetime as one of the world's safest and busiest high-speed rail lines.

Japan's capital city, Tokyo, is located on the middle bottom on the map and is part of the Kanto region, indicated by the lighest coloured dot. As the Shinkansen network expands into futher regions of Japan, the colour becomes darker.

From the northest to the southest prefectures with located stations, the total distance covered by passing Tokyo is roughly a whopping 2267 km.

The number of stations grouped by regions could be determined by the size of each dot on the map. Larger dots indicate more stations are located in that particular region compared to the smaller sized dots.

Interestingly, even though Tokyo could be considered as the "Central" of the Shinkansen Network, the region that contains the most amount of station is in the Tohoku region, with a total of 33 stations compared to only 15 in the Kanto region.

Most of the Shinkansen operations are managed by the Japan Railways Group. The group consist of for-profit stock companies that are differentiated based on their area of operation. The territories are divided into Central, East, West, Kyushu, and Hokkaido

Japan is known as the pioneer for high-speed railways with the Shinkansen. Throughout the years, other countries saw the pontential and wanted to have high-speed trains of their own. Overall, there are more than 10 different trains that could cruise well over 250 km/h.

With the top 7 of the fastest amongst the trains listed, two of them are the Japanese made Shinkansens. The E5 Shinkansen is the earlier model that has been in service for a while. Meanwhile, the L0 Shinkansen is the newly developed one, which could reach a maximum speed of around 500 km/h, smoking other high-speed railways currently in service.

Not only in terms of speed, Shinkansen also excels in terms of Passenger Capacity. Shinkansens, even the earlier model, could carry passengers of capacity around 700 people. In comparison, other high-speed trains on average could only carry around 500 people. Only the "CRH2C China Railways" train that closes the gap with around 600 people. Shinkansen still holds the crown for the most spacious high-speed railways in service.

Shinkansen of different lines would have different Passenger Ridership, Covered Distance, and even Top Speed. We wanted to find out if there are correlation between these three performance measures.

One observation we could determine is that the distance covered by the train is directly proportional to the top speed. If the distance covered by the train is large, then the train's speed would need to be faster to save time.

For the passenger ridership, we couldn't observe any regular pattern output in relation to the distance and speed. We got two observations in which the ridership is high, but for Joetsu, both the distance and top speed are low, and the opposite is true for Tokaido.
